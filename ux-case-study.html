UX Case Study — Self-Directed

Redesigning the mobile product page for Elevato Studios

An audit-and-redesign proposal for the site I already maintain day to day — built to show how I diagnose friction in an experience, not just fix what's broken.

RoleUX Audit & Redesign
ToolsFigma, Shopify Admin, GA4
TypeSelf-directed / concept
Siteelevatostudios.com
A note on honesty: this redesign has not shipped to production. It's a self-directed exercise built on a real site I manage, meant to demonstrate process — not a claim of measured business results.
01

Discover

Elevato Studios is the live Shopify storefront for Style Without Effort, a direct-to-consumer apparel brand. I own this site's day-to-day content, QA, and analytics. A routine mobile QA pass turned into a full heuristic evaluation of the product page — the highest-traffic, highest-stakes screen on the site.

I walked the mobile product page against Nielsen's usability heuristics and flagged four recurring friction points:

Visibility of system status

No swipe indicator

The product image gallery is swipeable, but nothing signals that or shows how many photos remain.

Recognition over recall

Size guide is buried

Sizing help is a small text link beneath the size buttons — easy to miss on a phone screen.

User control & freedom

No persistent add-to-cart

The CTA sits in the normal content flow below the description, so it scrolls out of reach.

Match with the real world

Social proof arrives too late

Review count and rating live at the very bottom of the page, after the buying decision is usually made.

02

Define

User goal: decide on a size and buy with confidence, fast. Business goal: reduce mobile bounce and friction between product view and add-to-cart.

I set one constraint for the redesign: every change has to remove a tap, a scroll, or a moment of doubt — not add decoration.

03

Ideate

I sketched three directions before committing to one, ranging from a minimal patch to a fuller restructure.

A — Sticky CTA onlyAdd a persistent add-to-cart bar, leave everything else as-is.
B — CTA + earlier reviewsSticky CTA, plus pull the rating badge up near the price.
C — Full restructureSwipe dots, earlier social proof, bigger size targets, sticky CTA.
→ Chosen direction
04

Design

Direction C addressed all four audit findings at once without adding new screens or steps — just reordering and clarifying what was already there.

Before
1No swipe indicator on the image gallery
2Size guide is a small, easy-to-miss link
3Add-to-cart scrolls out of reach
4Reviews arrive after the decision point
After
1Dot indicators show position in the gallery
2Size guide is a visible pill button with clearer targets
3Add-to-cart bar stays pinned at the bottom
4Rating moves up next to the price, before the size decision
→

Why move the rating up: social proof works best right before a decision point, not after it. Sizing is the biggest hesitation moment on an apparel PDP, so the rating now sits directly above it.

→

Why a sticky CTA: on mobile, the add-to-cart action shouldn't depend on how far someone has scrolled. Making it persistent removes a scroll-back tax with no added visual weight.

→

Why bigger size targets: the original 18px touch targets sit below common mobile tap-target guidance. Sizing them up reduces mis-taps without changing the layout's footprint.

05

Validate & reflect

This case study stops at a validated design direction, not a shipped result — and I'd want to keep it that way in how it's presented. The honest next steps:

1

Run a small moderated usability test (5 mobile users) watching specifically for hesitation at the sizing step.

2

Ship the sticky CTA and reordered rating as a scoped A/B test, since they're the two lowest-risk, highest-impact changes.

3

Measure against add-to-cart rate and mobile bounce rate — the two metrics tied directly to the original audit findings.

Why this matters for how I work

I already own this site's front end, so this case study doesn't have to stop at a Figma file. I could implement this redesign directly in Liquid, CSS, and JavaScript — the same skill set I use to maintain the live site today.
